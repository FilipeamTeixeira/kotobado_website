The algorithm

FSRS v5 — the most accurate open scheduling algorithm

Traditional SRS apps like Anki's default scheduler use SM-2, a formula from 1987. It works, but it treats all learners identically and uses a fixed forgetting curve.

FSRS (Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler) was developed in 2022 using machine learning on millions of real reviews. It models three variables per card — stability, difficulty, and retrievability — and shows each card precisely when your recall probability drops to your target threshold.

Peer-reviewed at ACM KDD. Used by Anki (as an optional algorithm), RemNote, and now Kotobado.

Three variables per card

Stability
How long until you forget it. Grows with every successful recall.
Difficulty
Intrinsic hardness of the card. Learned from your own review history.
Retrievability
Your current recall probability. Decays continuously between reviews.

Kotobado also adjusts scheduling based on your pre-flip confidence rating — a second signal that catches overconfidence, the most common cause of SRS failure.

The neuroscience

Features designed around how memory actually works

01

Pre-flip confidence rating

Before seeing the answer, you rate your confidence. This forces genuine retrieval rather than recognition — a fundamentally different and stronger memory process. It also trains metacognition: over time, you learn when you actually know something versus when you only think you do.

Kornell, Hays & Bjork, 2009 — testing effect research

02

Interleaved front-face rotation

Each card is shown from a randomly chosen field — kanji, kana, sentence, or radicals. The same word enters your memory through different angles, encoding it across multiple neural pathways. You won't recognise a word in conversation but fail to produce it.

Taylor & Rohrer, 2010 — interleaved practice

03

Reconsolidation booster

Every time you correctly recall a card, its memory trace becomes briefly unstable for ~45 minutes — a window where a second encoding is disproportionately strengthening. Kotobado surfaces these cards again in that window as a dedicated booster queue.

Nader et al., 2000 — memory reconsolidation

04

Production typing mode

Once a card is established (5+ reviews), Kotobado occasionally asks you to type the reading rather than choose it. Generating an answer is harder than recognising one and creates a stronger memory trace. The generation effect is one of the most replicated findings in memory research.

Slamecka & Graf, 1978 — generation effect

05

Overconfidence penalty

When you say "I know it" but then fail — the most dangerous failure mode in SRS — Kotobado penalises stability more aggressively than a standard failure. Standard Anki never catches this. The hypercorrection effect shows these surprises encode corrections most deeply.

Butterfield & Metcalfe, 2001 — hypercorrection effect

06

Contextual example sentences

Every card includes a real example sentence as a dedicated review field. Memory research consistently shows that words encoded in grammatical context — activating the hippocampus alongside surrounding meaning — are retained significantly longer than isolated symbols.

Godden & Baddeley, 1975 — context-dependent memory

Features

Everything you need.
Nothing you don't.

A focused set of tools built around the review loop. No gamification noise. No streaks punishing you for missing a day.

Four-field cards

Every card carries kanji, kana + romaji, an example sentence + romaji, and radicals + mnemonic. The app chooses which field to show as the question, hiding the others until you flip.

Calibration scatter plot

After 50 reviews, a personal fingerprint appears — a scatter plot of your confidence against your actual recall. Dots below the diagonal are your overconfident failures. It changes as you improve.

Flow state ring

A 2-pixel border around the session container shifts from green (optimal 80–95% accuracy) to blue (too easy) to amber (too hard). It exists in your peripheral vision, never demanding attention.

CSV import

Load your own card deck from a CSV file directly from your phone. Bring any word list — your textbook vocabulary, a JLPT frequency list, anything. Cards work offline immediately after import.

Offline-first

Review cards on the train, in class, anywhere with no signal. Everything runs on-device. Ratings sync silently when you reconnect. Nothing requires a server to study.

Review frequency control

Adjust the target retention threshold — Relaxed (80%), Balanced (90%), or Intensive (95%). One setting changes all intervals globally. Study at your own pace without touching any FSRS parameters.

How it works

One review loop, engineered for depth

01

Card appears

The app randomly selects which of the four fields to show as the question. The others stay hidden. You can't predict which angle you'll be tested from.

02

Rate your confidence

Before seeing the answer — No clue, Vague idea, or I know it. This forced attempt is the retrieval. The rating alone changes how the memory is encoded.

03

Flip and see all fields

The full card reveals — kanji, kana, romaji, example sentence with romaji, radicals, and mnemonic. Everything at once, in context.

04

Rate your actual recall

Blackout, Hard, Good, or Perfect. FSRS uses both ratings together to compute the next interval. If your confidence and recall don't match, the algorithm notices.
